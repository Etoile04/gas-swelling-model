# Kinetics of fission-gas-bubble-nucleated void swelling of the alpha-uranium phase of irradiated U-Zr and U-Pu-Zr fuel

# 1. Introduction

Fuel elements for the Integral Fast Reactor (IFR) under development at Argonne National Laboratory consist of metallic U-Zr and U-Pu-Zr alloy pins sealed in austenitic stainless steel cladding tubes [1,2]. The normal operating temperature range for the fuel is 673-1173 K. At temperatures below 935 K, swelling of the U-10Zr and U-xPu-10Zr fuel during irradiation displays many features that are analogous to the swelling caused by void formation in nonfissionable metals during fast-neutron or charged-particle irradiation. As shown in fig. 1, large cavities form in the fuel in a highly distorted microstructure at temperatures below 935 K, where the orthorombic α-phase forms the matrix of the alloy with δ-lamellae present below 890 K [1]. The fuel microstructure consists of an α-uranium, low-Zr solid solution phase and a low-uranium, high-Zr phase (UZr2 δ-phase). At the beginning of irradiation, most of the fuel is in the α/δ-phase field. Swelling begins after an exposure threshold is reached (fig. 2), with all compositions behaving in a similar manner with the exception of 0% Pu, which swells significantly faster and reaches a somewhat higher level of axial growth [1].

Previous attempts at modeling gas release and swelling in IFR fuels have focused on gas-driven swelling of bubbles [3]. The observed microstructure of the predominantly α-uranium zone consists of an extensive “tear-like” porosity that can be interpreted as a relatively coarse structure of cavities extending along original grain boundaries, as well as a finer laminar structure of porosity existing along the α/δ-phase boundaries within the grains (fig. 1). This finer, “swirled-worked” structure is here ascribed to cavitational void swelling, i.e., the bias-driven swelling of voids. The larger irregular tears (grain-boundary tearing) is a stress-induced process that can account for a large amount of swelling, but is not considered in this

Because fuel swelling and gas release are interrelated, it is essential that the kinetics of fission-gas release from the α-uranium zone be consistent with the fuel swelling kinetics, e.g., those of cavitational-void swelling. Experimentally [2], the quantity of gas released has a nearly linear dependence on burnup once the initial incubation period has passed. The fractional gas release, shown in fig. 3, increases rapidly with burnup and exhibits asymptotic behavior that approaches fractional releases of 70-80%. Because the porosity structure in the α-uranium zone is so dense, a gas atom produced by fission has a high probability of escaping from the fuel (i.e., the fuel regions are thinner than the average distance that a fission-produced gas atom must travel before it comes to rest). Inasmuch as the α-uranium zone occupies about 70% of the fuel volume, this release behavior could be understood if the fractional gas release from the α-uranium zone approached 100%.

Gas bubbles that are formed during the incubation period act as potential nuclei for cavities. Examination of the micrograph shown in fig. 1 reveals that the cavities are, in general, associated with the phase boundaries. Bubble growth on normal high-angle grain boundaries is enhanced due to the reduced effectiveness of irradiation-induced re-solution [5-8]. Re-solution is thought to have a reduced effect on boundary bubbles (compared to intragranular bubbles) because the boundary recapture distance is relatively long; the average distance traveled by an ejected atom is within the steep portion of the vacancy and gas atom concentration gradients, and the atom is quickly recaptured by the bubble. In what follows, the assumption is made that, due to the reduced effectiveness of irradiation induced re-solution, enhanced bubble growth also occurs on the $\alpha/\delta$-phase boundaries.

During the incubation period, gas bubbles formed on the $\alpha/\delta$-phase boundaries are transformed into cavities, and the cavities undergo bias-driven growth. Gas release does not occur to any great extent during this period because interconnection of the porosity is incomplete. After the incubation, the cavities grow and begin to interconnect, providing pathways for the gas to reach open surfaces and vent into the plenum [7,8]. Subsequently, gas generated within the $\alpha$-uranium is released directly into the interconnected cavity region; this leads to a linear dependence of quantity released on burnup. The combination of the above two processes will lead to asymptotic behavior for the fractional gas release.

It is theorized here that the swelling mechanisms at work in the $\alpha$-uranium zone can be modeled by single-vacancy and single-interstitial kinetics, with interphase gas bubbles providing the void nuclei, thus avoiding the use of complicated defect-interaction terms required for the calculation of void nucleation. This model for cavitational void swelling will allow calculation of the transition between bias-driven growth (voids) and gas-driven growth (bubbles). The full set of constituent equations that form the basis of the cavitational void swelling material is described in section 2. Results obtained with the model (henceforth called Cavity) are compared with U-10Zr and U-19Pu-10Zr deformation data in section 3, and with high purity uranium swelling data in section 4. The effect of various materials properties on the calculated quantities is discussed in section 5. Finally, a discussion of results and conclusions from this study are presented in section 6.

# 2. Cavitational-void swelling model

The construction of a mechanistic model for gasbubble-nucleated cavitational-void swelling of the $\alpha$ uranium zone is presented in this section. The physics of the model is based on bias-driven growth (by adsorption of excess vacancies produced by irradiation damage) of phase-boundary cavities that initially evolve as fission-gas bubbles. The description of fission-gas release from $\alpha$-uranium is supported by photomicrographs (e.g., see fig. 1) obtained by scanning electron microscopy of postirradiation U-xPu-Zr fuel specimens that portray this zone as a laminar structure (microstructure forms at temperature) with a high degree of interconnected porosity. Based on examination of the $\alpha$-uranium microstructure, the thin fuel lamina is approximated by a spherical grain with a 1-$\mu$m radius. Gas generated within the fuel lamina diffuses to the phase boundaries and is released if a network of long-range interconnected porosity has been established. Growth of cavities within the $\alpha$-uranium lamina is not predicted to occur because the effect of irradiation-induced gas atom re-solution is strong and prevents these gas bubbles from growing to the critical size required for bias-driven growth. In addition, the reduced effect of re-solution on phase-boundary bubbles (i.e., the boundaries act as an efficient sink for gas atoms) allows gas bubbles that nucleate on the phase boundaries to grow to critical size, depending on the fuel temperature.

In the following discussion it is assumed that the gas bubble/cavity distribution within the lamina and on the phase boundaries can each be represented by an appropriate density of average-size cavities. Here, a cavity can either be a gas bubble, or a void. The problem is to calculate the evolution of the intra and interphase cavities and to provide a criterion for evaluating bias-driven growth of cavities as compared to gas-driven growth. Fission gas generated within the matrix of the U-Pu-Zr alloy fuel evolves according to the equation

$$
\frac{dC_{g}^{b}} {dt} = -16 \pi F_{n}^{b} R_{c}^{b} D_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b} - 4 \pi R_{c}^{b} D_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b} C_{c}^{b}
-\dot{g}_{0}(t) + \beta \dot{f} + B N_{c}^{b} C_{c}^{b}
\tag{1}
$$

where the terms on the right-hand side represent the loss of gas atoms due to bubble nucleation, diffusion of fission gas to existing cavities, diffusion of gas to phase boundaries, and the gain of gas due to generation by fissioning uranium atoms and gas-atom re-solution from gas bubbles, respectively (the superscript b denotes quantities located in the fuel bulk, i.e., within the fuel lamina). $F_{n}^{b}$ is the nucleation factor (i.e., probability that when two atoms come together they form a stable gas-bubble nucleus). $R_{c}^{b}$ is the bulk gas-bubble (cavity) radius, $D_{g}^{b}$ is the gas-atom diffusivity, $C_{g}^{b}$ and $C_{c}^{b}$ are the gas-atom and gas-bubble (cavity) concentrations, $B$ is the irradiation-induced gas-atom re-solution rate from bubbles, $N_{c}^{b}$ the number of gas-atoms in a cavity,


$\beta \dot{f}$ is the gas-atom generation rate, and the loss rate of gas-atoms to the phase boundaries $\dot{g}_0(t)$ is given by

$$
\dot{g}_0(t) = -\frac{6}{d} \frac{D_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b}}{\partial r} \bigg|_{r=d/2}
\tag{2}
$$

where $r$ is the radial coordinate, and $d$ is the diameter of the equivalent spherical grain. Eq. (2) is solved by splitting the grain into two concentric regions of approximately equal volume. In each region, the gas concentration is represented by a quadratic function of the radial coordinate. The gas-filled cavities within the bulk material evolve according to the equation

$$
\frac{dC_{c}^{b}}{dt} = 16 \pi F_{\text{n}}^{b} R_{c}^{b} D_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b} / N_{c}^{b}.
\tag{3}
$$

The term on the right-hand side of eq. (3) represents the increase in bulk cavities due to cavity nucleation.

Conservation of gas atoms requires that

$$
N_{c}^{b} C_{c}^{b} + C_{g}^{b} + \int_0^t \dot{g}_0(t) \, dt = \int_0^t \beta \dot{f}  dt
\tag{4}
$$

Differentiating eq. (4) and substituting in eqs. (1) and (3) results in an expression for the time rate of change of the number of gas atoms in an average-size bulk cavity,

$$
\frac{dN_{c}^{b}}{dt} = 4 \pi R_{c}^{b} D_{g}^{b} C_{g}^{b} - B C_{c}^{b}
\tag{5}
$$

Gas reaching the phase boundaries evolves according to the expressions

$$
\frac{dC_{g}^{f}}{dt} = -16 \pi F_{\text{n}}^{f} R_{c}^{f} D_{g}^{f} C_{g}^{f} C_{g}^{f} - 4 \pi R_{c}^{f} D_{g}^{f} C_{g}^{f} C_{c}^{f} + \dot{g}_0(t)- \dot{h}_0 C_{g}^{f}
\tag{6}
$$

and

$$
\frac{dC_{c}^{f}}{dt} = 16 \pi F_{\text{n}}^{f} R_{c}^{f} D_{g}^{f} C_{g}^{f} C_{g}^{f} / N_{c}^{f}
\tag{7}
$$

where the superscript f denotes quantities associated with the phase boundaries (e.g., faces), and $\dot{h}_0$ is the fission gas-release rate. Conservation of gas atoms on the phase boundaries requires that

$$
N_{c}^{f} C_{c}^{f} + C_{g}^{f} = \int_0^t \dot{g}_0(t) \, dt - \int_0^t \dot{h}_0(t) \, dt
\tag{8}
$$

Gas release from the phase boundaries, which occurs when long-range interconnection of the interphase porosity develops is represented by the last term on the right-hand side of eq. (8). The fission-gas release rate $\dot{h}_0$ is given by

$$
\dot{h}_0 = \chi V \left[ C_{g}^{f} + C_{c}^{f} N_{c}^{f} \right]
\tag{9}
$$

where $V$ is the volume of the fuel under consideration, and $\chi$ is the fraction of boundary cavity interlinkage, which is determined as follows.

Phase-boundary cavity interlinkage is determined by directly addressing the distribution of the calculated cavity size. The projected areal coverage of the phase-boundary face by these cavities, per unit volume, is given by

$$
A_{f} = \pi R_{c}^2 C_{c}^{f} f_{f}(\theta)
\tag{10}
$$

where $f_{f}(\theta)$ is a geometrical factor that accounts for any deviation from a spherical shape of the cavities (e.g., lenticular). If the cavities are assumed to be closely packed, the maximum areal coverage per unit area of phase boundary is $A_{f}^{max} = 0.907$. Cavity interlinkage occurs when

$$
A_{f} \geq A_{f}^{max} S_{v}^{aa}
\tag{11}
$$

where $S_{v}^{aa}$ is the grain-face area per unit volume.

Eqs. (10) and (11) do not account for local variations in fuel microstructure. To include these effects in the calculation of cavity interlinkage, it is assumed that the local variations in fuel microstructure can be represented by the width $\sigma_{f}$ of a distribution of $A_{f}$, eq. (10), such that the fraction of cavity interlinkage is given by

$$
\chi = \frac{1}{\sigma_{f} \sqrt{2 \pi}} \int_{x = A_{f}^{max} S_{v}^{aa}} \exp \left[ \left( -x - A \right)^2 / 2 \sigma_{f}^2 \right] dx
\tag{12}
$$

The width of the distribution in eq. (12) is a function of erratic structural parameters that depend on local fuel condition and heterogeneity. In principle, this width can be determined experimentally.

In analogy to the derivation of eq. (5), with the aid of eqs. (6)–(8), we can obtain an equation for the time rate of change of the number of gas atoms within an average-size phase boundary cavity, i.e.,

$$
\frac{dN_{c}^{f}}{dt} = 4 \pi R_{c}^{f} D_{g}^{f} C_{g}^{f} - \dot{h}_0 N_{c}^{f}
\tag{13}
$$

In a material containing several types of point-defect sinks, such as dislocations and cavities, the sinks generally have different capture efficiencies for vacancies and interstitials [9]. During irradiation, partitioning of point defects to sinks is unequal. This bias results in a larger flux of vacancies to voids and of interstitials to dislocations. Because the thermal emission rate of vacancies from cavities depends on the exponential of the inverse of the cavity radius, at a given temperature and for a fixed number of gas atoms in the cavity, there is a critical cavity radius above which the bias-induced net vacancy influx is larger than the net thermal vacancy outflux, and the cavity will either shrink or, if it contains gas, become stabilized. Similarly, because the thermal emission rate of vacancies also depends on the exponential of the inverse of the temperature, at a given cavity radius and for a fixed number of gas atoms in the cavity, there is a critical temperature below which the bias-induced net vacancy influx is greater than the net thermally emitted vacancy outflux. As the temperature is raised, the critical radius increases.

Based on the above discussion, the growth rate of a cavity, as determined by the net vacancy flux, can be expressed as
$$
dR_c/dt = \frac{1}{4\pi R_c^2 C_c} \left[ k_{vc}^2 D_v c_v - k_{ic}^2 D_i c_i - k_{vc}^2 D_v c_v^*(R_c) \right]
\tag{14}
$$
where $R_c$ is the cavity radius, $C_c$ the cavity concentration, $D_v$ and $D_i$ are the vacancy and interstitial diffusivities, respectively, $c_v$ and $c_i$ are the respective concentrations of vacancies and interstitials, $k_{vc}^2$ and $k_{ic}^2$ denote the capture efficiency of a cavity for a vacancy or interstitial, respectively, and $c_v^*(R_c)$, the thermal equilibrium vacancy concentration near a cavity of radius $R_c$, is given by
$$
c_v^c(r_c) = c_v^0 \exp \left[ - \left( P_g - 2\gamma/r_c - \sigma \right) \Omega / k T \right]
\tag{15}
$$
where $P_g$ is the pressure of the gas in the cavity, $\Omega$ is the atomic volume, $\gamma$ is the surface energy, $\sigma$ is the hydrostatic stress due to external forces (e.g., the cladding), and $c_v^0$ is the defect-free thermal equilibrium fractional vacancy concentration given by

$$
c_v^0 = \exp \left( - \epsilon_v^F / k T \right)
\tag{16}
$$

where $\epsilon_v^F$ is the vacancy formation energy.

The pressure of the gas in the cavity, $P_g$, can be determined from the number of gas atoms in the cavity, $N_c^I$ or $N_c^B$, and an appropriate equation of state; a modified hard-sphere equation of state is utilized in the present formulation [10]. Once the fuel contacts the cladding, the high value of $\sigma$ generated will inhibit further fuel swelling (see eqs. (14) and (15)).

The concentration of mobile defects within the $\alpha$-uranium (e.g., the vacancy concentration) can be determined from the solution of the rate equations describing point defect behavior. The equations are
$$
\frac{dc_v}{dt} = \phi \dot{f} - k_v^2 D_v c_v - \alpha c_i c_v
\tag{17}
$$
$$
\frac{dc_i}{dt} = \phi \dot{f} - k_i^2 D_i c_i - \alpha c_i c_v
\tag{18}
$$
Here, $\alpha$ is the rate constant for the bulk recombination of interstitials and vacancies, $\phi \dot{f}$ is the defect production rate (in displacements per atom (dpa) per second), and $k_v^2$ and $k_i^2$ are the total sink strengths for vacancies and interstitials, respectively.

As was mentioned in the introduction, on the basis of previous analytical studies [11] that used the defect behavior code Cluster 78 [12], only single-vacancy and single-interstitial kinetics are considered. Cavity nucleation results from the agglomeration of two (or more) gas atoms in the presence of one or more vacancies or vacancy clusters and is described by the first terms on the right-hand side of eqs. (3) and (7). If the only sinks for the points defects are cavities and dislocations, $k_v^2$ and $k_i^2$ are written
$$
k_v^2 = k_{vc}^2 + k_{vp}^2
\tag{19}
$$
$$
k_i^2 = k_{ic}^2 + k_{ip}^2
\tag{20}
$$

and the quantities $(k_v)^{-1}$ and $(k_i)^{-1}$ are the mean distances a free interstitial or vacancy moves in the material before it becomes trapped. The central problem in the rate theory of microstructural evolution is the determination of the various sink strengths for migrating point defects. The appropriate sink strengths of cavities for vacancies $k_{vc}^2$ and interstitials $k_{ic}^2$ in the continuum approximation implicit in a rate-theory approach take the form
$$
k_{vc}^2 = 4\pi R_c C_c [1 + k_v R_c]
\tag{21}
$$
and
$$
k_{ic}^2 = 4\pi R_c C_c [1 + k_i R_c]
\tag{22}
$$
The sink strengths of dislocations for vacancies and interstitials take the form
$$
k_{vp}^2 = Z_{vp} \rho
\tag{23}
$$
and
$$
k_{ip}^2 = Z_{ip} \rho
\tag{24}
$$
where $Z_{vp}$ and $Z_{ip}$ are the dislocation bias terms for vacancies and interstitials, respectively, and $\rho$ is the total dislocation density in the material. The full set of eqs. (1)–(24) that make up the fission-gas-bubble-nucleated cavitational swelling model is called Cavity.


A relevant question concerning the above formulation is what distinguishes cavities that reach a critical size on the phase boundaries from those within the α-uranium lamina. Bubbles on phase boundaries grow to larger sizes than those within the lamina because the effect of gas-atom re-solution is reduced, e.g., the term containing $ BC_{c}^{b} $ in eq. (1). Physically, this result is interpreted as due to the shallow depth of gas-atom penetration. On average, ejected gas atoms remain within the influence of the boundary and are quickly recaptured. For this reason the effects of gas-atom re-solution from boundary bubbles are neglected in the above formulation.

It is of interest to quantify the capability of Cavity to predict both gas-bubble-driven swelling (overpressurized cavities) and cavitation-void swelling (underpressurized cavities), as well as the transition from one swelling mechanism to the other. This is easily accomplished by simultaneously monitoring the cavity growth rate (eq. (14)) and the excess pressure $ P_{ex} $, which is defined as
$$
P_{ex} = P_{g} - 2\gamma/R_{c} - \sigma 
\tag{25}
$$
$ P_{ex} $ is in the argument of the exponential in eq. (15) for the thermal vacancy concentration near a cavity of radius $ R_{c} $. If the cavity growth rate is positive and $ P_{ex} $ is positive, the cavity (bubble) is overpressurized and grows by a gas-driven mechanism (i.e., the overpressure in the cavity due to the gas produces a stress field, emanating from the cavity, which is the driving force for pumping vacancies from the lattice into the bubble). On the other hand, if the cavity growth rate is positive and $ P_{ex} $ is negative, the cavity (void) is underpressurized and grows by a bias-driven growth mechanism (i.e., by accumulation of excess vacancies moving down a concentration gradient). The transition from gas-driven to bias-driven growth, for example, is predicted by observing $ P_{ex} $ move from positive to negative values, during which time the cavity growth rate remains positive.

# 3. Comparison with data from U-10Zr and U-19Pu-10Zr fuel elements

Incubational swelling is generally not observed in the case of pure metals. The incubation observed in the bias-driven swelling of stainless steels is quite short (10–20 dpa) compared with that observed in IFR fuels (hundreds of dpa) and is still not properly understood. Inasmuch as the critical cavity size required for void growth is relatively small (on the order of nanometers), this size is achieved for interphase bubbles at ~0–20 dpa, depending on the values of various gas-atom and microstructural properties, far short of the incubation time observed in the IFR fuels (e.g., see fig. 2). However, the results of Cavity (i.e., eqs. (1)–(24)) assessment has shown that long incubation times can be fully understood in terms of a reduced gas-bubble nucleation rate on the α/δ-phase boundaries. In general, gas-bubble nucleation in bulk material is thought to be inefficient due to a successful nucleation even requiring the presence of one or more vacancies or vacancy clusters in close proximity to the colliding gas atoms [8]. In contrast, gas bubble nucleation on normal high-angle grain boundaries is usually treated as 100% efficient due to the characteristic high-sink strength nature of the boundaries. # 5. Effects of materials properties on calculated swelling

The values of many materials properties used in the Cavity calculation of U-10Zr and U-xPu-10Zr irradiation behavior have not been experimentally determined. Thus, a “best guess” methodology based on physical intuition has been employed for selecting physically realistic values for use in the Cavity simulations. Because of this, it is important to assess the effect of changes in the value of a property on the calculated results. In this way, a delineation of the most important properties can be established to optimize future experimental efforts aimed at measuring properties.

# 6. Discussion and conclusions

Swelling of the $\alpha$-uranium phase of uranium alloy fuel for the IFR has been interpreted as fission-gas-bubble-nucleated cavitational void swelling. The physics of the model is based on bias-driven growth (by adsorption of excess vacancies produced by irradiation damage) of grain-boundary cavities that initially evolve as fission-gas bubbles. Growth of cavities within the bulk material is not predicted to occur because the effect of irradiation-induced gas-atom re-solution is strong and precludes the growth of intragranular gas bubbles to the critical size required for bias-driven growth. Because the effect of gas-atom re-solution on phase-boundary bubbles is reduced (i.e., it is assumed that like normal high-angle grain boundaries the phase boundaries act as an efficient sink for gas atoms), gas bubbles that nucleate on the phase boundaries can grow to critical size, depending on the fuel temperature. The model can predict both gas-bubble-driven swelling (overpressurized cavities) and cavitational-void swelling (underpressurized cavities), as well as the transition from one swelling mechanism to the other by simultaneously monitoring the cavity growth rate. (eq. (14)) and the excess pressure $P_{\text{ex}}$ (eq. (25)). If the cavity growth rate is positive and $P_{\text{ex}}$ is positive, the cavity is overpressurized and the cavity (bubble) grows by a gas-driven mechanism (i.e., the overpressure in the cavity due to the gas produces a stress field emanating from the cavity, which is the driving force for pumping vacancies from the lattice into the bubble). On the other hand, if the cavity growth rate is positive and $P_{\text{ex}}$ is negative, the cavity is underpressurized and the cavity (void) grows by a bias-driven growth mechanism (i.e., by the accumulation of excess vacancies moving down a concentration gradient). The transition from gas-driven to bias-driven growth, for example, is predicted by observing $P_{\text{ex}}$ move from positive to negative values while the cavity growth rate remains positive.

Another important difference between bias and gas-driven growth is in the relationship between gas release and swelling. For bias-driven growth, the swelling phase occurs as the cavities located on the phase boundaries undergo growth due to an influx of vacancies. Eventually, long-range interconnection of this porosity occurs and fission gas is released into the sodium that fills the fuel–cladding gap (sodium logging of the interconnected porosity can occur, but is not considered in this paper). Subsequently, fission gas, which is being generated within the fuel matrix, enters the interconnected porosity and is released; because of the continual influx of vacancies, the fuel continues to swell as gas is released from the fuel (e.g., see fig. 5). For gas-driven growth, fuel swelling continues until long-range interconnection of the porosity occurs. Subsequently, venting of fission gas from the porosity limits further swelling (i.e., gas release reduces the gas pressure within the porosity, and thus the driving force for continued swelling). This observation underlies the quite different relationship between gas release and swelling for gas-driven (i.e., bubbles) and bias-driven (i.e., voids) swelling. Gas release leads to a reduction in fuel swelling (i.e., a zero or negative swelling rate) for gas driven swelling, whereas for bias-driven growth, the swelling rate remains positive.

Another difference in behavior between gas-driven and bias-driven swelling occurs under thermal annealing conditions. For gas-driven swelling, annealing at temperature in the absence of an irradiation environment leads to continuation of fuel swelling that is due to the continued accumulation of fission gas in bubbles by gas-atom diffusion. This type of swelling is enhanced by the removal of the irradiation field, which is responsible for gas-atom re-solution from bubbles. On the other hand, thermal annealing of fuel that has undergone bias-driven growth leads to a decrease in fuel swelling. This is demonstrated in fig. 16, which shows Cavity-calculated bias-driven swelling for U–10Zr at 673 K for a 10000-h irradiation ($\approx 1$ at\% burnup) followed by a 10000-h anneal in the absence of irradiation at the peak swelling temperature (873 K). As shown in fig 16, the fuel swelling decreases during the annealing period. The reason for this decrease in swelling is the reduction in the vacancy concentration due to the turning off of the irradiation field. Damage produced by irradiation results in a supersaturation of vacancies that are available for void growth. In the absence of irradiation, the vacancy concentration approaches the concentration set by thermal equilibrium. Because this vacancy concentration is lower (for the temperatures shown in fig. 16) than the vacancy concentration at the surface of the cavity, thermal emission of vacancies from the cavities is initiated.
